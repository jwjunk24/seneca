The Borrowed Yardstick

Meditations, Books 5 and 10

In Book 5 of the Meditations, Marcus Aurelius gives himself an exercise he returns to throughout the work: when another person's behavior disturbs you, ask whether you have not made comparable mistakes, in the same circumstances or in different ones. The point isn't self-punishment. It's a reality check on the confidence of the verdict. In Book 10, he adds: "People exist for the sake of one another. Teach them then or bear with them." This looks like patience, a counsel of tolerance. But the first option is harder than it sounds. Teaching implies that you've accurately understood what the person is missing, and why they're missing it, and that this understanding is reliable enough to offer correction. That's a great deal to assume before the case has fully come in.

What Marcus keeps running up against in the Meditations is the gap between the standards he carries and the standards others have been shaped by. He was trained by Stoic teachers, given decades of reflective practice, formed in particular ways by particular encounters. He knows this. And yet in the daily friction of governing — the courtier who flatters rather than advises, the general who calculates for advantage, the colleague who deflects rather than engages — there's a recurring temptation to measure them against what he has come to value and find them simply wanting. The Meditations are partly a record of catching this mid-measurement and asking: where did this standard come from? Is it mine specifically? And if it's mine, what gives it authority over someone else's life?

The standard any person carries is partly chosen and partly formed — built over years of particular teachers, particular failures, particular recoveries. When applied to someone else, the implicit assumption is that the same formation was available to them, or that they've made a deliberate choice to fall short. Often neither is true. The colleague who doesn't prioritize honesty in the way you do may have been formed in an environment where directness was genuinely dangerous. The friend who deflects rather than engages may have found, across a very different history, that engagement produces harm as reliably as understanding. The standard that feels self-evident arrived through a specific route. The person being measured by it may never have traveled that route at all.

Simone Weil, in "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God," locates the problem with unusual precision. True attention, she argues, requires something like emptying: "The soul empties itself of all its own contents in order to receive into itself the being it is looking at, just as he is, in all his truth." Measuring brings the standard to the person — the evaluator arrives fully loaded, framework already assembled, and the other person gets fitted into it. What Weil is describing is the opposite movement: arriving with as little as possible already decided, before any framework has been applied. She made this argument about helping a suffering friend and solving a geometry problem in the same breath, on the theory that the required discipline is identical. Before the solution, before the instruction, before the verdict: a quality of receptivity that requires setting aside what was already being carried.

"The soul empties itself of all its own contents in order to receive into itself the being it is looking at, just as he is, in all his truth."

Marcus doesn't resolve this tension — the Meditations are a record of returning to it rather than solving it. In Book 5, he reminds himself: "Receive without pride, relinquish without struggle." The shape this describes — holding something without clutching it — applies to evaluative frameworks as naturally as to anything else a person carries through life. And the person who falls short of a particular standard may not be failing so much as navigating. They have their own formation, their own pressures, their own account of what they were attempting that never surfaces in the verdict rendered about them from the outside.

The yardstick is real — what it measures is not therefore false. But a measure built for one person's life, applied to another person's, is an instrument working outside its calibration. What gets lost in the gap is exactly what the measurement can't reach: the formation behind the choice, the options that were actually available, the internal story that will never appear in the verdict.
