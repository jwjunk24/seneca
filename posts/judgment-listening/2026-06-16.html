Beginner's Ears

Letter 38

Letter 38 is one of Seneca's shortest, and one of his most precise. Lucilius has apparently asked for more letters — more arguments, more philosophy delivered at volume. Seneca pushes back. What actually changes a person, he says, is not the formal speech but the quiet conversation: the kind where two people are genuinely talking, not one performing at the other.

He makes the distinction in Latin between two words — the formal oration and the intimate exchange — but the point is simple: a lecture puts the listener's defenses up. The person on the receiving end knows they're being addressed, instructed, improved. They brace. The quiet conversation slips past that. And then Seneca reaches for an image that stops you:

"Seeds must be planted singly, that each may develop according to its own nature. A shower of words does not bring any of them to fruit."

He's writing about how to share ideas, but the implication runs the other direction too — toward how we receive them. A shower of words, whether coming out of your mouth or forming inside your head while someone else is still speaking, prevents anything from taking root. The listening mode and the broadcasting mode are mutually exclusive. You can be formulating your response, or you can be actually hearing what's being said. Rarely both at once.

This threads through other letters quietly. In Letter 40, Seneca is annoyed by a philosopher known for speaking at a gallop — the speed, he says, makes real thought impossible, for speaker and listener both. The fast talker isn't trying to connect; he's trying to overwhelm. And Letter 29 has him giving up on a particular correspondent because the man is too pleased with himself to receive anything. What goes unsaid there is the precondition for any useful conversation: someone has to actually be open to being surprised.

Shunryu Suzuki, the Zen teacher who helped bring Buddhism to the United States in the 1960s, put a version of this at the center of his teaching. His book Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind opens with a line that has become famous enough to be almost invisible: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few." He meant it about meditation, but it maps cleanly onto the act of listening to another person. The expert already knows what's coming. They've categorized this person, anticipated this argument, identified this pattern — and so they stop actually listening about thirty seconds before the other person stops talking. The beginner doesn't know yet. They're still open to what's actually there rather than what they've predicted will be.

The difficulty is that becoming an expert on the people around you feels like paying attention. You know them, you understand how they think, you can finish their sentences. That familiarity can be a form of care. But it can also be a way of replacing the actual person with a model of them — and then responding to the model rather than to what they just said. Seneca's seed image is useful here: the model of a person is soil you've already planted. There's no room for anything new to go in.

The moment to watch for is when the answer starts forming before the sentence is finished. That's not always a problem — sometimes you genuinely know where something is going. But sometimes it's the expert arriving early, closing the door. The question isn't whether to have a model of the people you know. It's whether you can hold it loosely enough that the real person can still surprise you.
