What Cannot Be Summed

Simone Weil, "Human Personality" (1943)

In 1943, Simone Weil wrote an essay called "Human Personality" — partly a response to the wartime language of rights, partly something more restless. Her trouble with rights-language was specific: rights attach to qualities. To the intelligent, the productive, the socially valuable. And what she kept encountering, in philosophy and in her attention to people who were suffering, was something that couldn't be located in qualities at all. "At the bottom of the heart of every human being," she writes, "from earliest infancy until the tomb, there is something that goes on indomitably expecting, in the teeth of all experience of crimes committed, suffered, and witnessed, that good and not evil will be done to him. It is this above all that is sacred in every human being." Not the good qualities. Something that has nothing to do with qualities.

The distinction sounds philosophical until it meets daily life. The colleague who is consistently thoughtless. The friend who makes the same mistake in every relationship. The relative who says the wrong thing at every gathering. These assessments may be accurate as far as they go — the quality is real, the pattern is real. What Weil is pointing at is the gap between the quality and the person who carries it. Thoughtlessness is not the whole person. And yet the habit of summing up — this is who she is, this is what he does — treats the quality as the person, and then responds to the category rather than to the actual individual standing there.

Later in the essay, Weil describes what it would mean to truly attend to someone in difficulty: "The love of our neighbor in all its fullness simply means being able to say to him: 'What are you going through?'" The question sounds obvious. What makes it hard is that most people encountering someone in difficulty already have an interpretation ready — they know what the problem means, what caused it, what should be done about it. The interpretation arrives before the question has been asked. What gets offered is not attention but a mirror: the listener's own framework, reflected back. The question "What are you going through?" requires holding that framework in suspension, and treating the other person's experience as genuinely unknown territory rather than as another instance of a story already filed.

The Talmud, in tractate Sanhedrin (4:5), arrives at a related point from an entirely different direction. Discussing the weight of testimony in capital cases, the rabbis make what seems like a detour into cosmology: unlike coins stamped from the same die, which come out identical, every human being stamped from the same original comes out different. "Therefore, each and every person is obligated to say: The world was created for my sake." The claim isn't about self-importance. It's about non-fungibility — the impossibility of treating any individual as simply an instance of a type. The person in front of you is not an example of their category. They contain, as the Sanhedrin passage puts it, an entire world.

What Weil and the Sanhedrin passage share is resistance to the completeness of any assessment. Not a denial that assessments exist or can be accurate — the unreliable person may well be unreliable, the careless person may genuinely be careless. But the quality, even when real, is not the final word. The person contains something the quality cannot contain, something invisible to the act of summing. And the practical implication — the one that actually changes how another person is treated — is that the verdict, however accurate, should remain provisional. Not because accuracy doesn't matter, but because the person will, at some point, exceed whatever has been decided about them.

The question Weil says one should be able to ask — "What are you going through?" — is harder than it sounds precisely because the answer often feels like it's already known. And the feeling of already knowing is exactly what closes the space where the actual person would otherwise be.
