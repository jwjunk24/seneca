Seneca — Letters to Lucilius

June 15, 2026

The Equal Soul

Letter 47

Sometime around 65 CE, Seneca sat down to write a letter that would have struck most of his contemporaries as genuinely strange. He was not a fringe figure — he had been the most powerful man in Rome behind Nero himself — and yet here he was telling Lucilius to invite his slaves to dinner. Not to serve at dinner. To sit down and eat.

Letter 47 is one of the most arresting documents to survive from the ancient world. Seneca opens it by telling Lucilius he has heard, with pleasure, that he talks with his slaves informally, as equals. "That's what your good sense suggests," he writes. Then he turns to the people who would find this scandalous and addresses them directly: "They are slaves, people say. But they are also fellow human beings." He means this not as sentiment but as metaphysics. The soul has no rank. Fortune assigns the body a master; it cannot touch what is inside.

"Show me a man who is not a slave: one is enslaved to lust, another to greed, another to ambition, and all men are enslaved to hope and fear."

The letter is full of concrete, almost uncomfortable detail. Seneca describes the Roman dinner party, where a slave standing nearby is forbidden to open his mouth — not to speak, not to cough, not to sneeze. Any murmur is punished with a beating. Guests gossip freely about their own household slaves while the men pouring their wine are expected to disappear into furniture. Seneca finds this contemptible. He wants Lucilius to picture the slave's actual interior life: the resentments stored up, the dignity suppressed, the person hidden inside the role. "Treat your inferior," he says, "the way you would want your own superior to treat you."

This idea doesn't appear only once. In Letter 31, Seneca argues that perfecting oneself means recognizing that nothing about a person's external condition — wealth, lineage, the office they hold — touches the question of whether they are good. Letter 5 makes the same move in the other direction: don't signal your philosophical seriousness through conspicuous austerity, because that, too, mistakes the surface for the substance. What matters in every case is what Seneca calls the same thing throughout: the soul. By Letter 77, as he contemplates his own death, this calculus feels urgent in a different way — if the inner life is what counts, then how we treat others' inner lives is where virtue actually lives or dies.

Bryan Stevenson has spent decades doing something that most people would find exhausting even to contemplate: sitting with men on death row — many of them poor, many of them Black, many of them wrongly convicted — and treating them as though their humanity were simply not in question. His book Just Mercy (2014) and the organization he founded, the Equal Justice Initiative, are the record of this practice applied at scale. What's striking about Stevenson's account is how plainly he describes the resistance he encounters — from courts, from prosecutors, from a public that has, in effect, decided certain people can be made furniture. His answer to that resistance is not primarily a legal argument. It's the same move Seneca makes: name the soul. Insist on seeing it. Refuse to let the category collapse the person.

Seneca was writing inside a society that considered slavery not just legal but natural, and he still found his way to: they are fellow human beings. The question worth sitting with isn't what he got right in spite of his world. It's which present-day equivalents of the slave standing silently at the wall we have simply learned not to see.
