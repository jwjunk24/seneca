The Hurried Verdict

Letter 40

In Letter 40, Seneca tells Lucilius about a philosopher named Serapio who had recently come to Rome. Serapio was brilliant, Seneca allows — but his speech was torrential. Words tumbled over each other, a rush without pause, an avalanche of conclusions. Seneca is not charmed. "Words that pour out faster than they can be caught," he writes, "cannot take hold in the listener, who turns away when he can find no foothold." The problem is not the ideas themselves. It is that nothing has been given time to land.

What Seneca notices about Serapio is really a portrait of a particular mental posture: one that has already finished thinking and is simply discharging its results. The speed is the tell. When someone knows what they want to say — and has already decided how it will be received — there is no need to slow down. There is nothing left to offer for consideration. The words fill the room, but the exchange is over before it begins.

The same speed operates in listening. Most conversations, examined honestly, involve two people each waiting for the other to finish. The pause that follows a sentence is not a space of reception — it is a runway for the response already prepared. Someone says something unexpected, or difficult, or out of character, and the listener has already filed it: sorted under familiar, stamped with a label, assigned to a category of person who says such things. The verdict arrives before the testimony is complete.

"The speech that treats of truth should be unaffected and plain. This hurried style touches the feelings of a large audience but does not teach."

Seneca's point is about oratory, but it reaches further. Teaching, here, stands for any genuine transmission — not just of ideas but of a person. The orator who rushes is performing; the listener who rushes is also performing, enacting the appearance of understanding while the actual person in front of them goes unseen.

Marcus Aurelius returned to this problem in the Meditations with a diagnostic precision that feels almost clinical. In Book 11, working through a set of practices for responding to someone who has acted wrongly, he includes this: consider whether you yourself have not done the same thing, in the same circumstances or different ones. The exercise only makes sense if you've actually heard what the person did — not what you assumed they did, not what it reminded you of, not the type it fit, but the specific act. But Marcus knew the temptation. Earlier, in Book 9, he notes that when another person's behavior provokes irritation, the irritation is information about the observer as much as the observed. To be irritated is to have already decided. The verdict is in. The person standing there is almost beside the point.

The difficulty is that this hurry feels like wisdom. Reaching a quick conclusion about someone does not feel like rashness — it feels like pattern recognition, like the accumulated knowledge of how people tend to work. Serapio's speed felt, presumably, like eloquence. Seneca's objection is subtle: it is not that speed is always wrong, or that slowness is always right. It is that genuine contact — with an idea, with a person — requires a moment of suspension, a pause in which something can actually arrive.

There is a specific sensation, if one pays attention, that marks the moment the mind stops receiving and starts deciding. Something closes. It is almost audible — an inner click. The question is whether it is possible, with practice, to notice the click before it happens, and hold the door open a little longer.
