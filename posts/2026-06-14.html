The Timely Exit

Letter 77

One afternoon near Puteoli — the great bay south of Naples — Seneca watched a fleet of Alexandrian grain ships glide into harbor. They were identifiable from far off by their linen topsails, spread wide while every other vessel had furled theirs against the wind. The crowd along the quay rushed to see them. Seneca didn't move. He had arrived, he tells Lucilius, at a point where the arrival of ships no longer felt like something worth running toward. It's a small detail, but it sets the mood of Letter 77 exactly: a man who has stopped being pulled around by the world's ordinary excitements, thinking about what really matters now.

What follows is one of Seneca's most unnerving and honest letters — a sustained meditation on how long is long enough to live, and whether a life's value can be measured in years at all. His position is blunt: it cannot. "A life is not short if it contains all that is necessary," he argues. And if it contains everything necessary, then the moment of departure carries no special tragedy, and its timing is not really the point. He reaches for the image of theater:

"The play has been well enough acted; but whether the third or the fourth act is to be the last depends upon Him who ordered this drama of ours, not upon you. You have nothing to do with the length of this term."

What he's trying to dislodge is the reflexive assumption that more time is always better — that a life cut short is always a tragedy, and a long one always a gift. He offers a counterexample that must have stopped readers cold even then: a German gladiator, being transported to the arena in a cart, feigned sleep and then drove his own head into the spokes of the turning wheel, killing himself rather than face the degrading performance awaiting him. Seneca doesn't present this as despair. He presents it as dignity — a man choosing the terms of his exit when every other term had been stripped from him.

The theme of departure runs quietly through many of the letters. In Letter 12, Seneca walks through a crumbling childhood villa and notices that the plane trees he planted are now massive, the gatekeepers old, the house itself collapsing — and he finds in this decay not grief but a kind of companionship. Everything ages together. In Letter 26, written when he himself is old, he describes watching his body slow while his mind stays quick, and approaches the coming end with something close to curiosity: what will the final transition actually feel like? In both letters the posture is the same — look at the exit directly, without flinching, without rushing toward it or inventing reasons to look away.

David Bowie understood this. In 2015, while privately dying of liver cancer, he spent his final eighteen months recording Blackstar, released on his 69th birthday — two days before he died. His producer Tony Visconti confirmed afterward that Bowie had known it was his last record and had shaped it deliberately as a farewell. The album is spare, dissonant, almost confrontational — nothing like a victory lap or a greatest-hits tribute to himself. It was a craftsman choosing the terms of his departure while there were still terms to choose, turning the exit into something made rather than merely endured. Francis Whately's documentary David Bowie: The Last Five Years follows how it was built, and what Bowie seemed to be thinking in the time he had left.

What Seneca keeps circling in Letter 77 is not death as catastrophe but something he treats almost like a craft problem: how does a thing end well? Not dramatically, not passively, but with the same care you'd bring to anything you've been working on for a long time. The question isn't whether you'll leave. It's whether you'll leave well.
