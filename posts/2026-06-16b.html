The Fault You Find

Letter 50, with Letter 7 and Letter 29

Letter 50 opens with an act of charity that Seneca almost misses. He's describing a man who has only now, late in life, turned seriously toward philosophy — and there's a temptation in the story, the kind where you hold someone up as a cautionary example, a life half-wasted. But Seneca catches himself. "Why do we wonder that certain vices persist in some men?" he asks. And then the turn: we keep them going ourselves. The man who came to wisdom late is not so different from the man who came early. What matters is the turning.

That question keeps working beneath the surface of the letter. There's something in the habit of noticing others' flaws — their dishonesty, their cowardice, their endless inconsistency — that functions more as self-protection than genuine perception. Once you've named a fault, you're done. That person is now, definitively, a coward. A hypocrite. You've filed them, and their actual complexity can stop demanding your attention.

Seneca reaches this from another angle in Letter 7, writing about the Roman games and the crowd's appetite for brutality. His concern isn't only the violence — it's how easily moral judgment becomes its own kind of sport: you watch, you categorize, you feel superior, and in doing so you've absorbed the same spirit you were busy condemning. The judger and the crowd have more in common than the judger would like to think. Letter 29 is quieter about it: Seneca has given up on a man named Marcellinus, who has decided he no longer needs to learn anything. What stays with you isn't Marcellinus's arrogance — it's the certainty in Seneca's own verdict about what kind of person Marcellinus is.

Marcus Aurelius returns to the problem of other people more persistently than almost anything else in the Meditations. He reminds himself — with the kind of repetition that reveals a real struggle — that people who act badly do so from ignorance rather than malice, that they share his rational nature, that his irritation with them says something about him. "When you wake in the morning," he writes, "tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surly." The point isn't resignation. It's to arrive at the encounter without the self-righteousness that fires up when someone turns out to be exactly what you already feared they were. Gregory Hays's translation of the Meditations (Modern Library, 2002) strips away the Victorian stiffness and lets the urgency come through — this particular passage is in Book 2, and it reads less like philosophy than like a man genuinely trying to talk himself into being better.

The gap Marcus keeps pointing at is one Seneca knew too: we hold the full context of our own failures intimately. We were tired, afraid, working from incomplete information, having a bad month. Other people's failures look cleaner from the outside — they just look like failures, stripped of any mitigating story. The morning reminder isn't pessimism about humanity. It's a deliberate act of parity: extend to the people who frustrate you the same complexity you extend to yourself without even noticing you're doing it.

What's worth watching is where the verdict forms before the evidence is in — when you're less encountering a person than confirming a category you'd already built. The question isn't whether your assessments are sometimes right. It's whether you've left enough room for them to be wrong.
