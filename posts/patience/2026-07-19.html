Live the Questions

Letters to a Young Poet, Letter 4

In the summer of 1903, Rainer Maria Rilke was writing from a small farming village on the German moors to a nineteen-year-old military cadet named Franz Kappus. The young man had sent his poems and, underneath them, a harder question: whether to give his life to writing or to the army career already laid out for him. He wanted a verdict. What he received instead, in the fourth of the letters later collected as Letters to a Young Poet, was an instruction in how to wait.

Rilke tells him not to hunt for the answers:

Be patient toward all that is unsolved in your heart and try to love the questions themselves, like locked rooms and like books that are now written in a very foreign tongue. Do not now seek the answers, which cannot be given you because you would not be able to live them. And the point is, to live everything. Live the questions now.

The image worth holding onto is that last one. A question is not a problem sitting on a desk, waiting to be solved before the day can properly begin. It is a room a person lives inside, sometimes for years, and the living is what slowly furnishes it. Rilke's promise to the cadet is almost offhand and almost outrageous: that someone who can stay with an unanswered thing long enough will, without noticing the moment it happens, live along some distant day into the answer. The resolution does not arrive by being chased. It arrives by being outlasted.

This is the part patience gets wrong most often — mistaking itself for a delay in the search rather than a different way of searching. The cadet wanted Rilke to shorten the distance between the question and the reply. Rilke refused, because a reply handed over too early would only be a foreign word memorized, not a truth a person could actually stand on.

The old Chinese handbook of the way, the Tao Te Ching, sets down the same counsel in a single image in its fifteenth chapter: Who can wait quietly while the mud settles? Who can remain still until the moment for action comes? Stir a pool of muddy water and it stays clouded; the harder the stirring, the longer the murk. Leave it alone and the sediment drops of its own weight, and the water clears without anyone having cleared it. Some questions are like that water. The wish to see the bottom right now is precisely the motion that keeps the bottom hidden.

Kappus never became the poet he hoped to be, and Rilke seems to have sensed early that he would not. Yet the counsel held anyway — because it was never really about arriving at the right career. It was about whether a person can bear to hold a question open long enough for a real answer to form, instead of grabbing a false one just to end the discomfort of not yet knowing.
