The Three Graces

On Benefits, Book 1

There is an old picture that Seneca pauses over near the start of On Benefits, his long treatise on the giving and receiving of good things. The three Graces are painted as sisters, young and smiling, dressed in loose transparent robes, and they stand hand in hand in a ring. He asks why the artists arranged them so, and then answers his own question. They are linked because a kindness moves from one person to another and, passing from hand to hand, still comes back at last to the one who first gave it. They dance because the whole thing is meant to be joyful. Their robes are loose and flowing because a benefit should be given freely, holding nothing back.

The heart of the image is the ring itself. As Seneca puts it, the order of the dance must never be broken, since the beauty of the whole is destroyed if the sequence is interrupted anywhere, and it is loveliest when it runs on unbroken and keeps its turn. A gift given but never received, or received but never acknowledged, is a circle with a gap in it — three sisters with one hand hanging empty, the music stopped. What completes the figure is not the grand return of some equal favor but simply the second sister taking hold of what is offered.

This is where the practice hides. Seneca says elsewhere in the same book that the person who receives a benefit gladly has already paid back its first installment. Gratitude, on this view, is not a debt that hangs over the head but the very act that finishes the gift. To notice what has been handed over, to take it with open pleasure rather than suspicion or embarrassment, is to close the person's fingers around the offered hand and let the dance turn.

G.K. Chesterton pushes the thought one step further out. In Orthodoxy he keeps returning to the sense that existence itself arrives as a present no one asked for or earned, and that the fitting first response is thanks — his test of all happiness, he says, is gratitude. A birthday gift is not wages; it is given before any deserving. If Chesterton is right, then the first Grace has already extended her hand long before anyone thinks to look. The morning, the ordinary working body, the people who are simply there — these are held out and waiting. Nothing more is required than to reach back and take them.

The ring only breaks at one point: the hand that will not close. To be ungrateful, on this reading, is not to be wicked so much as to have quietly stopped dancing. So the question worth carrying into the day is a small one — which hands are extended right now, patient and unremarked, waiting only to be taken?
