The Seasoning of Hunger

Letter 123

Seneca arrives at his villa late, worn out from a bad road, and finds nothing ready: no cook fussing over dinner, no fresh bread, none of the comforts a tired traveler might reasonably expect. Stale bread is all there is. And yet he eats it happily, because, as he tells Lucilius, hunger itself is the seasoning. What makes a meal good is not the meal but the appetite brought to it, and the appetite is always available, regardless of what the pantry holds.

The letter is not really about bread. It is about training the mind to stop auditing circumstances for what they lack and start noticing what they already supply. Seneca had grown suspicious of a household staffed with people whose only job was to manufacture delight — the chef who could make plain food taste rare, the servant whose whole purpose was anticipating a whim before it formed. That kind of life, he says, teaches a person to need more and more just to feel the same amount of pleasure. The stale loaf did the opposite. It asked for nothing extra and gave back real satisfaction, because he came to it with an appetite instead of a checklist of requirements.

This is a habit of attention as much as a habit of eating. Most days contain far more ordinary sufficiency than anyone bothers to register — a working pair of legs, a room that keeps out the weather, a friend who answers the phone — and all of it goes unnoticed precisely because it never announces itself as a delicacy. G.K. Chesterton made nearly the same point from a different angle in Orthodoxy, arguing that gratitude is the correct default posture toward existence itself, not a mood reserved for lucky days. His line has outlasted the book it came from: "the world will never starve for want of wonders, but only for want of wonder." Chesterton thought children understood this instinctively — a child who asks for the same story every night isn't bored by repetition but delighted that the sun rose again exactly as it did yesterday. Adults lose that hunger for the ordinary and then wonder why so little satisfies them.

Put the two side by side and a fuller picture appears. Seneca supplies the discipline: strip away the manufactured extras and see what plain provision actually tastes like. Chesterton supplies the disposition: treat the ordinary as something that could easily not have been there at all. Neither is asking for less to happen. Both are asking for a sharper appetite — the kind that finds the stale loaf, the working legs, the answered phone, worth noticing in the first place.

The pantry rarely changes overnight. What changes, when it changes at all, is whether a person walks in hungry or walks in already full of complaints about what isn't there.
