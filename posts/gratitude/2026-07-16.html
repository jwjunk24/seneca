A Reason Ahead

Man's Search for Meaning

Viktor Frankl, in Man's Search for Meaning, noticed a pattern among the prisoners of the camps that had nothing to do with physical strength. The ones who broke first were not the weakest in body but the ones who had let go of the future. He describes a fellow prisoner who dreamed that liberation would arrive on a fixed day in spring. When that day came and the gates stayed shut, the man's fever climbed, and within days he was dead. What had killed him was not only the sickness already in his blood but the collapse of the thing he had been living toward.

Frankl borrowed a line from Nietzsche to name what he was watching happen:

He who has a why to live can bear almost any how.

A why was no luxury in that place; it was the load-bearing wall. A task left unfinished, a book still to be written, a person waiting at home — any of these could hold a life upright through conditions built to crush it. Frankl himself clung to a lost manuscript he was determined to rewrite, and imagined standing one day at a lectern, describing the very suffering he was inside of, turning the present horror into something a future self would calmly examine and explain. Good expectation, it turns out, is not a decoration laid over a good life. Sometimes it is the reason there is a life at all.

Most days ask for far less than this and offer far more. The habit worth building is the one that leans, even slightly, toward what is coming rather than bracing against it — the quiet assumption that something ahead is worth reaching for. This is not the same as denial. Frankl was the last person to pretend the camp was anything other than what it was. Hope of this kind looks the facts in the eye and still keeps a door propped open.

Seneca arrives at nearly the same place from the far end of a life. In the twelfth of his letters to Lucilius, aging and tired, he visits a country house he has owned for years and finds everything crumbling — the plane trees bare, the walls giving way, and a worn-out old man at the door who turns out to be a boy he once played with. The easy response would be complaint. Instead he decides that age, like the last pressing of the season, carries its own sweetness, and that the closing years are a harvest rather than a robbery.

It is with life as with a play; it matters not how long the action is spun out, but how good the acting is.

What waits ahead, even the parts that wear the face of decline, still holds fruit for anyone willing to expect it.

Both men point at the same small muscle. The future stays unwritten either way; the only real choice is which posture to face it in. The one who keeps a reason out in front — a task, a person, a harvest, a why — is not being naive. That one has simply understood which direction to lean, and stands ready to meet whatever the next day happens to bring.
