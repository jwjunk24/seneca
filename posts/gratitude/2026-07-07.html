The Debts Worth Keeping

Meditations, Book 1

Marcus Aurelius opens the Meditations in a way almost nobody expects from a book of philosophy: not with an argument, but with a list. The entire first book is a private catalogue of debts, one name after another, each paired with the exact thing that person gave him. From his grandfather Verus, good manners and control of temper. From his mother, piety and generosity, and a way of living far removed from the habits of the rich. From his teacher Rusticus, the habit of reading carefully instead of skimming for approval, and the discipline of writing plain letters instead of showy ones. He is not thanking people in general terms. He is naming what specifically each one left in him, as though gratitude only becomes real once it gets that precise.

It is worth remembering who is writing this. Marcus Aurelius ran an empire, fought wars on its borders for most of his reign, and buried several of his own children. He had every excuse to open his private notebook with a complaint. Instead he opens it with an inventory of what had been given to him, compiled, as far as anyone can tell, purely for his own use — these were notes to himself, never intended for an audience. That changes how the list reads. It is not a performance of thankfulness. It is a man doing the quiet, unglamorous work of keeping track, apparently because he had found that tracking it did something for him that complaining never could.

G.K. Chesterton, writing in Orthodoxy, pushes this same instinct further out until it stops being about people at all. In the chapter he calls "The Ethics of Elfland," he argues that the correctly oriented mind treats ordinary existence itself the way Marcus treats his grandfather and his mother: as something given, not something owed. "The world will never starve for want of wonders," he writes, "but only for want of wonder." Marcus keeps a ledger of teachers. Chesterton wants a ledger of everything — the specific fact of trees, the specific fact of daylight, the oddity that there is a world at all rather than nothing, treated with the same startled, itemized attention usually reserved for an unexpected gift.

Most people could write their own version of Marcus's list without much trouble — a name, a thing they gave, one line each. The harder and more interesting habit is Chesterton's: to keep going after the names run out, and put down what the morning itself supplied before anyone else touched it.
