What You Would Have Prayed For

Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, Book 7

In the Meditations, Marcus Aurelius returns repeatedly to a practice that cuts against the grain of ordinary ambition. Book 7 contains one of his more striking variations on it:

Do not indulge in dreams of having what you have not, but reckon up the chief of the blessings you do possess, and then thankfully remember how eagerly you would have craved them, had they not been yours.

The test he is proposing is a simple one: go back to an earlier version of yourself, the one for whom these things were still only hoped-for, and ask what that person would have given to have what is now taken for granted. The food on the table, the quiet morning, the person sleeping nearby, the use of a body that works — these were once on the list of things most desired. They arrived. And then, slowly, they became the scenery, invisible in the way that only the constant and present can become invisible.

The exercise has a surprising effect. Seen through the eyes of the person who did not yet have them, ordinary things show up as extraordinary again. Not because anything changed, but because the angle of vision shifted. Marcus calls this kind of deliberate recollection a form of discipline — not sentimental gratitude for its own sake, but a correction to the perceptual drift that comfort produces. Given enough time, almost any good fortune begins to feel like the baseline. The practice of remembering what was once only hoped-for interrupts that drift.

Marcus was not a man untouched by good fortune — he was emperor, which complicates any advice he gives on contentment. But the Meditations was a private document, written for himself and not intended for circulation, and his repeated return to this theme suggests it was genuinely difficult for him. Rank and power are capable of distorting perception in a particular way: they make what is extraordinary feel ordinary, and what is ordinary feel insufficient. The habit of recalling what he once hoped for was his own antidote to that distortion.

The Tao Te Ching arrives at the same problem from a different direction. Chapter 44 opens with a series of questions: "Fame or integrity: which matters more? Money or happiness: which is more precious? Success or failure: which is more destructive?" The questions are not rhetorical in a cheap sense — they are meant to be sat with. The chapter closes with what may be the most compact formulation of the sufficiency problem in any tradition: "Know when to stop, and you will not wear out." Laozi is not counseling passivity; the knowing referred to is active, an ongoing practice of noticing when pursuit has outrun the thing being pursued. Without that noticing, the engine runs past its destination.

What Marcus and the Tao Te Ching share is the recognition that sufficiency is not a level to be reached — it is a perception to be cultivated. The things that constitute a rich life are often already present. The difficulty is not acquiring them but seeing them as what they are, rather than as what habit has flattened them into.

The gratitude that arrives on its own tends to be thin and occasional. The gratitude that results from deliberately recalling what one once hoped for goes deeper — not because the circumstances changed, but because the angle of vision did.
