The Slight Wants of Nature

Letter 17

In Letter 17, Seneca is writing to Lucilius, who has offered a familiar excuse for postponing philosophy: he is not yet rich enough to afford the leisure for it. Seneca has no patience for this. Poverty, he insists, is not an obstacle to wisdom but a fairly natural condition for pursuing it, since wisdom asks so little of the body to begin with. He reminds Lucilius of the state everyone starts in: "No man is born rich. Every man who emerges into the light of day is commanded to be content with milk and rags." That is the whole of a newborn's estate, and it was sufficient. Everything acquired after it was surplus, added on top of a foundation that never actually required it.

The line Seneca is building toward is the one people still quote without knowing where it came from: "You will never be poor if you live according to nature; you will never be rich if you live according to opinion, for nature's wants are slight, but the demands of opinion are boundless." Two different accounting systems are being proposed here. One is set by the body: food enough to end hunger, shelter enough to keep off weather, rest enough to restore the limbs. The other is set by comparison, by what the neighbor has, by what the culture has decided a person of a certain standing ought to display. The first account can be closed. The second cannot, because its balance is never fixed by anything as steady as a stomach or a roof. It moves whenever someone else's account moves.

Seneca is not inventing this distinction. He is borrowing it, and he says as much elsewhere with a kind of good humor about raiding a rival school for useful material. The idea belongs originally to Epicurus, whose Letter to Menoeceus sorts human wanting into three bins: desires that are natural and necessary, desires that are natural but not necessary, and desires that are neither natural nor necessary, existing only because opinion has invented them. Hunger is the first kind. A taste for one dish over another is the second. The craving for a fortune large enough to be envied, or a name that outlives the body, belongs to the third, and Epicurus is blunt about what it costs: these desires are hard to satisfy and possibly dangerous to pursue, precisely because nothing in nature set a limit on them. A Stoic quoting an Epicurean on this point is itself a small argument for the idea. Whatever the schools disagreed about, they agreed that the wanting which ruins people is the kind that was never actually assigned by nature in the first place.

Somewhere back at the start, milk and rags were enough. Nothing about the body has changed since then, only the ledger it's been handed.
