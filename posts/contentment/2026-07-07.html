Everything Since Then Is Gain

Letter 17

In Letter 17, Seneca is writing to Lucilius, who has offered a familiar excuse for postponing philosophy: not yet rich enough, not yet settled, better to wait. Seneca brushes this off, and the way he does it is worth sitting with, because underneath the argument is a genuinely cheering fact. He reminds Lucilius where every person starts: "No man is born rich. Every man who emerges into the light of day is commanded to be content with milk and rags." That was the full estate on offer at the beginning, and it was enough to live on. Everything that arrived after it, every meal beyond hunger, every roof better than bare shelter, every year beyond the first, is something added on top of a baseline that never actually promised any of it. Looked at that way, a person's life stops reading as a list of shortfalls and starts reading as a long run of unrequested gifts.

Seneca's famous line follows from this: "You will never be poor if you live according to nature; you will never be rich if you live according to opinion, for nature's wants are slight, but the demands of opinion are boundless." The usual way to hear this is as a warning against wanting too much. But there is a brighter reading sitting right next to it. If nature's asking price was always this low, then the distance between what was owed and what has actually been received is enormous, and that distance is pure surplus. Measured against opinion's shifting scoreboard, a person can always find a reason to feel behind. Measured against milk and rags, almost everyone is already living far ahead of schedule.

Epicurus makes a companion point in his Letter to Menoeceus that pushes the same optimism further. He sorts wanting into natural-and-necessary, natural-but-unnecessary, and empty desires invented by nothing but opinion, and then makes an observation that gets skipped over too quickly: the necessary things are also the easiest things to get. Bread satisfies hunger about as well as a feast does, he points out, and satisfaction, once measured correctly, turns out to be close at hand rather than perpetually out of reach. That is an argument for good cheer as much as for restraint. The good life was never hiding at the far end of a long pursuit. By his account and Seneca's both, it was available roughly where a person was already standing.

The rags were the whole bill. Whatever has shown up since then, however plain it looks some mornings, was never actually owed — which makes it a strange kind of luck to be walking around still receiving it.
