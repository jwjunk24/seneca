The Raft We Carry

Majjhima Nikaya 22 (Alagaddupama Sutta)

The raft simile in the Alagaddupama Sutta, the twenty-second discourse of the Majjhima Nikaya, is one of the most economical warnings in Buddhist teaching. A traveler reaches a large body of water and builds a raft to cross it. Having crossed, he thinks: this raft was so useful — perhaps he should lift it onto his head and carry it with him wherever he goes. The Buddha offers this image to make a point about doctrine: no teaching, however helpful in getting from one shore to another, should be carried as a possession once the crossing is done. The raft is a tool, not a prize. Clinging to it on dry land isn't devotion. It's a different kind of confusion.

The simile was given about religious views, but it maps with uncomfortable precision onto the views people hold about each other. At some point, someone formed an understanding of a particular person: this is how they are, this is what they tend to do, this is what to expect. The understanding may have been accurate when it formed. The raft crossed the water. But then years passed, the person changed, circumstances shifted — and the raft is still being carried on the head. The relationship continues, but partly as a conversation with the portrait, not with whoever is now standing there.

What makes the image useful rather than abstract is that carrying the portrait doesn't feel like clinging. It feels like knowing someone. The certainty that comes with long familiarity feels like earned wisdom, not like stiffness. "I know how she handles conflict." "He always deflects when things get difficult." "That's just how she is." These sentences aren't dangerous because the observations behind them are false. They're dangerous because accurate observation of the past is being used to pre-answer the present. The raft was real. The water was real. But the far shore is not the near shore, and the person who now stands on it is not quite the person who got on the raft.

Seneca does something uncommon in Letter 34. Writing to Lucilius, he says: "I claim you for myself; you are my own work." He means it not as a fixed label — not you were my project and this is what you became — but as a recognition of ongoing change. Lucilius is still developing, and Seneca has been watching closely enough to track it. "I see the promise which I watched developing come to fruition." What's quietly remarkable is that this requires Seneca to not already know what he will see. The statement of pride is also a statement of genuine attention: the kind where the other person can still surprise you, because the image of them hasn't been sealed. The portrait, for Seneca, keeps getting revised. That's not uncertainty about Lucilius — it's the opposite of uncertainty. It's the result of actually looking.

The Alagaddupama Sutta doesn't romanticize the raft. The teaching is not that building it was a mistake, or that the crossing was an illusion. The raft was necessary. What happened on the near shore mattered. The point is only about what comes after — whether the tool that served the crossing becomes an object carried for its own sake, out of habit or sentiment or the simple comfort of having something to hold. Applied to people, this means: the understanding that was built has value. The question is whether it's still in service of seeing the person, or whether it's now a possession — familiar, comfortable, and no longer quite pointing at the person who has kept on changing in front of you.

The raft is not abandoned — it's released, having done what a raft does. A person surprised you recently. The question worth sitting with is whether that surprise was welcome, and what the answer reveals about how tightly the portrait has been held.
